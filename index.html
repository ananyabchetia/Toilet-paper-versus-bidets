Flush out the truth:

Will you roll with toilet paper or bidets?

By: Ananya Chetia

Shooting water to clear the rear remains unusual because majority of Americans roll with toilet paper.

One alternative to just wiping with paper is the bidet. The device comes in various shapes with one purpose, to clean one's buttocks and surrounding area with water. Sometimes the bidet is a hand-held spray attached to the toilet that uses a home's water supply. Other times, it is a separate bowl that looks like a sink for one to sit. This is the type of bidet 65-year-old Amy Guskin saw thirteen years ago in a rented apartment in Paris.

"We just didn't use it. We weren't used to using them," Guskin said. She now carries a portable bidet whenever she travels.

The pandemic left panicked consumers to purchase toilet papers in bulk and several newsrooms reported on a national shortage. At Wegmans, employees limited how many toilet rolls Guskin and her husband could purchase. That's when Guskin made the call, it was time to get a bidet.

"Five years, it's never failed us. And it does warm water as well," she said.

The couple got the Brondel's SouthSpa option, a bidet attachment to the left of the toilet with the nozzle hooked at the back of the toilet bowl that sprays water. Grueskin said she and her husband can't go back to just using toilet paper after the bidet is set up. "How would you remove ketchup from a wall? With paper or would you actually clean it?" Guskin said.

A report from Cleveland Clinic suggests bidets are cleaner than toilet paper because "water can top a few squares of dry toilet paper in removing trace amounts of fecal matter after you poop." A 2022 study also found bidets reduced microbial contamination in fingers compared to those using toilet paper after pooping.

Bidets were most searched on Google in March 2020, at the start of the pandemic. There was a boost in bidet sales that year too, said Jason Ojalvo, former CEO of bidet company TUSHY for the LA Times. Ojalvo predicted that the high bidet sales “could be the tipping point that finally gets Americans to adopt the bidet.”

“The reality is, once you use a bidet to clean after pooping you cannot go back to wiping and toilet paper. You've learned too much! Wiping seems not just inefficient, but also barbaric, by comparison,” the former CEO said during the pandemic.

Ojalvo is not wrong, the bidet industry has grown and is set to be at $30.56 billion by 2025. But that is a timid value compared to the $120.49 billion toilet paper industry.

Joseph Gayetty invented the first commercially available roll of toilet paper in 1857 and toilet paper has left its stain on the United States since. The product first became highly popular when corporation Hoberg Paper sold Charmin toilet paper with the “Charmin lady” logo in 1928 and later the “Charmin baby” to emphasize toilet paper's softness. The product's legacy in American bathrooms can explain why many rely on wiping with paper, despite water being commonly used to clean other parts of the body.

The preference for toilet paper is not unanimous. Activists argue toilet paper is partially responsible for the deforestation and high emission rates in Canada's boreal forests. Major shareholders in the toilet paper industry, like Procter & Gamble, Kimberly-Clark and Costco rely on the forest's virgin wood pulp to produce softer and more absorbent toilet paper.

If all toilet paper were made with virgin wood pulp, the U.S would need to cut down over 65.5 million trees annually for consumers, according to a 2022 report by environmental impact consultancy Edge.

The study also found that cutting down trees for toilet paper in the U.S. can prevent forests from absorbing 17 million metric tons of carbon dioxide each year—an amount of emissions equal to a gas-powered car driving 43 billion miles.*

Procter & Gamble's CEO Jon R. Moeller said the company will improve environmental sustainability without compromising the company's economic performance.

“Sacrificing performance for environmental sustainability does not delight consumers and does nothing to create a more sustainable business or planet,” Moeller said at last fall's 2024 Investor Day in Cincinnati.

Law firm Hagens Berman filed a class-action lawsuit in January accusing the company of greenwashing and misleading toilet paper consumers, despite its role in damaging Canada's boreal forests.
